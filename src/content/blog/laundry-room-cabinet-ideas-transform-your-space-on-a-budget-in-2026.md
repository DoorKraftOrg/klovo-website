## 5 Laundry Room Layouts: Finding Your Perfect Configuration

Not all laundry rooms are created equal. The space you're working with—whether it's a compact closet or a dedicated 10x12 room—fundamentally shapes your storage strategy. Understanding your layout type helps you select cabinets and storage solutions that maximize functionality without overwhelming the space. Let's explore five common laundry room configurations and the cabinet setups that work best for each.

### 1\. Small Closet Laundry (Stackable + Side Cabinet)

**Typical Dimensions:** 3-4 feet wide × 4-6 feet deep (12-24 square feet)

The small closet laundry is increasingly common in apartments, condos, and compact homes. This configuration typically features a stacked washer-dryer unit (or a compact front-loader with a dryer above) in a closet or alcove. The challenge here is maximizing storage in minimal square footage while maintaining access to the appliances.

**Recommended Cabinet Setup:** A stackable washer-dryer unit leaves wall space on either side. Install a narrow wall cabinet (12-18 inches wide) on one side for detergents, fabric softeners, and stain removers. Above the stacked unit, a shallow wall cabinet (8-10 inches deep) provides storage for dryer sheets, lint traps, and cleaning supplies. If space permits, a small base cabinet (18-24 inches wide) beside the stacked unit offers storage for bulk supplies and cleaning tools. The key is vertical utilization—every inch of wall space becomes functional storage.

**Storage Capacity:** This configuration typically provides 15-25 cubic feet of storage, enough for a household's regular laundry supplies, cleaning products, and backup detergent. The stackable unit itself often includes a small shelf or storage area, adding another 2-3 cubic feet.

### 2\. Galley Laundry Room (Parallel Walls)

**Typical Dimensions:** 5-7 feet wide × 8-10 feet long (40-70 square feet)

The galley configuration features appliances and storage on opposite walls, creating a corridor-like space. This layout is common in homes where the laundry room is part of a hallway or between other rooms. The parallel wall setup creates natural zones for different functions: one wall for washing/drying, the opposite wall for storage and folding.

**Recommended Cabinet Setup:** On the appliance wall, install base cabinets flanking the washer and dryer (or beneath them if they're stacked). These base cabinets provide storage for heavy items like bulk detergent, cleaning supplies, and laundry baskets. On the opposite wall, create a folding station with a 24-30 inch deep countertop supported by base cabinets below. Above the countertop, install wall cabinets for linens, fabric softeners, and supplies. This creates a complete workflow: wash on one side, fold and store on the other.

**Storage Capacity:** A galley configuration can accommodate 40-60 cubic feet of cabinet storage, plus countertop workspace. This is sufficient for a family of 4-5 with room for bulk supplies, seasonal items, and backup inventory.

### 3\. L-Shaped Laundry Room

**Typical Dimensions:** 6-8 feet × 8-10 feet with an L-shaped footprint (48-80 square feet)

The L-shaped layout offers more flexibility than a galley. Appliances typically occupy one leg of the L, while storage and folding space occupy the other leg. This configuration works well when the laundry room is in a corner or when you want to create distinct zones for different activities.

**Recommended Cabinet Setup:** Position the washer and dryer on one leg of the L, with base cabinets underneath and wall cabinets above. On the other leg, create an extended folding counter (36-48 inches long) with base cabinets for storage below and wall cabinets above. If the L-shape allows, consider adding open shelving on the inside corner for frequently used items like dryer sheets, lint rollers, and stain removers. This creates a natural workflow where you move from washing to folding to storage without backtracking.

**Storage Capacity:** L-shaped rooms typically provide 60-90 cubic feet of storage capacity, making them ideal for larger households or those who like to keep backup supplies on hand.

### 4\. Dedicated Laundry Room (8×10 or Larger)

**Typical Dimensions:** 8-10 feet × 10-12 feet (80-120+ square feet)

A dedicated laundry room—one that serves no other purpose—offers the most flexibility and storage potential. These rooms often feature a full wall or multiple walls available for cabinetry, plus space for a large folding table, sorting station, or even a sink.

**Recommended Cabinet Setup:** In a dedicated laundry room, you can implement a comprehensive storage system. Install full-height cabinetry (base + wall cabinets) along one or more walls. Create a large folding counter (48-60 inches) with substantial base cabinet storage beneath. Add a utility sink if plumbing allows—this becomes invaluable for hand-washing delicates, treating stains, and cleaning supplies. Incorporate a sorting station with labeled bins for whites, colors, and delicates. Wall-mounted drying racks or a retractable clothesline add functionality without consuming floor space. The key is creating distinct zones: washing (appliances), treating (sink area), sorting (bins), folding (counter), and storage (cabinets).

**Storage Capacity:** A dedicated laundry room can provide 100-150+ cubic feet of storage, plus countertop and floor space for specialized equipment. This is ideal for large families, households with special laundry needs, or those who like to maintain extensive backup supplies.

### 5\. Combined Laundry/Mudroom

**Typical Dimensions:** 8-12 feet × 10-14 feet (80-168 square feet)

Modern homes increasingly combine laundry functionality with mudroom storage, creating a multi-purpose space that handles laundry, outerwear storage, shoe organization, and seasonal item storage. This hybrid approach maximizes the utility of a single room.

**Recommended Cabinet Setup:** Divide the space functionally. Dedicate one wall or corner to laundry (washer, dryer, folding counter, laundry-specific cabinets). Use the remaining walls for mudroom functions: hooks for coats and bags, cubbies for shoes, and cabinets for seasonal items. Use modular cabinetry that allows you to customize each section. For example, the laundry section might feature base cabinets with a countertop and wall cabinets above, while the mudroom section features open cubbies, hooks, and closed storage for items you want hidden. This dual-purpose approach requires careful planning but delivers exceptional functionality.

**Storage Capacity:** Combined laundry/mudroom spaces can provide 150-200+ cubic feet of total storage, with 60-80 cubic feet dedicated to laundry-specific items and the remainder for mudroom functions.

## Storage Solutions by Room Size: Matching Cabinets to Your Space

Beyond layout, the total square footage of your laundry room determines which storage solutions are practical and cost-effective. Let's break down storage strategies by room size, from compact apartments to spacious dedicated laundry rooms.

### Small Laundry Rooms (Under 25 Square Feet)

In ultra-compact spaces, every inch counts. Vertical storage becomes your primary strategy. Wall-mounted cabinets and shelves maximize storage without consuming floor space. Over-washer cabinets—shallow units that mount directly above a front-loading washer—provide convenient access to frequently used items while keeping them out of the way. Corner shelving units utilize otherwise wasted space. Magnetic strips on cabinet doors hold metal cleaning tools. Hooks on the inside of cabinet doors store cleaning cloths and lint rollers. The goal is to eliminate any unused wall or door space.

In these tight quarters, choose cabinets with clean lines and light finishes to avoid making the space feel cramped. Open shelving can work if items are neatly organized and visually appealing; closed cabinets are often better for hiding clutter and creating a sense of spaciousness. Consider a narrow folding board that mounts to the wall and folds down when needed, rather than a permanent countertop that consumes precious floor space.

### Medium Laundry Rooms (25-50 Square Feet)

Medium-sized laundry rooms offer more flexibility. This is where you can comfortably install both base cabinets and wall cabinets, creating a balanced storage system. Base cabinets provide storage for heavy items like bulk detergent, cleaning supplies, and laundry baskets. A countertop folding area (24-30 inches deep, 36-48 inches wide) becomes practical and genuinely useful. Wall cabinets above the countertop store linens, fabric softeners, and supplies. This configuration creates a complete laundry workflow without feeling cramped.

In medium rooms, you can also incorporate specialized storage: a dedicated shelf for stain removal supplies, a cabinet section for delicate-wash items, and perhaps a small utility sink if plumbing allows. The key is creating zones—washing zone (appliances), treatment zone (stain supplies, sink), folding zone (counter), and storage zone (cabinets).

### Large Laundry Rooms (50+ Square Feet)

Large dedicated laundry rooms allow for comprehensive, luxurious storage solutions. Full-wall cabinet systems become practical, providing 100+ cubic feet of storage. An island or peninsula with a large folding surface (48-60 inches) creates a central workspace. A sorting station with labeled bins for different laundry types becomes a permanent fixture rather than a temporary setup. Wall-mounted drying racks or a retractable clothesline add functionality. A utility sink becomes a valuable addition for hand-washing delicates and treating stains. Open shelving can display attractive storage baskets and bins without creating visual clutter.

In large rooms, you have the luxury of separating functions spatially. The washing zone (appliances) can be distinct from the folding zone (counter and island), which is separate from the storage zone (wall cabinets). This separation reduces congestion and makes the space feel organized and intentional.

## Cabinet Selection Guide: Choosing the Right Storage for Your Needs

Selecting laundry room cabinets requires understanding how different cabinet types serve different purposes. A well-designed laundry room typically incorporates multiple cabinet types, each optimized for its specific function.

### Why Moisture Resistance Matters

Before diving into cabinet types, it's essential to understand why moisture resistance is non-negotiable in laundry rooms. Laundry rooms are inherently humid environments. Washers and dryers release significant moisture during operation. Splashes from filling washers, spills from detergent bottles, and general humidity create a challenging environment for standard cabinetry. Moisture causes wood to swell, MDF to delaminate, and finishes to bubble and peel. In laundry rooms, moisture resistance isn't a luxury—it's a requirement for cabinet longevity.

### Base Cabinets for Heavy Items

Base cabinets form the foundation of laundry room storage. These sturdy units sit on the floor and support heavy loads: bulk detergent bottles, cleaning supplies, laundry baskets, and other weighty items. Base cabinets typically feature adjustable shelves, allowing you to customize shelf height based on what you're storing. A 36-inch wide base cabinet provides substantial storage in a compact footprint. Two or three base cabinets create a complete storage wall.

When selecting base cabinets, prioritize durability and moisture resistance. Look for cabinets with sealed surfaces that resist humidity and splashes. Adjustable shelves should be sturdy enough to support 50+ pounds per shelf without sagging. Consider cabinets with soft-close doors—they're more durable than standard hinges and create a more refined aesthetic.

### Wall Cabinets for Linens and Supplies

Wall cabinets mount above base cabinets or directly on walls, providing storage for lighter items: linens, fabric softeners, dryer sheets, and specialty cleaning products. Wall cabinets keep frequently used items at eye level and within easy reach. A 30-36 inch tall wall cabinet above a base cabinet creates a complete storage wall. Multiple wall cabinets can span an entire wall, providing comprehensive storage without consuming floor space.

Wall cabinets should be securely mounted to wall studs to support their weight and the weight of their contents. Choose cabinets with adjustable shelves so you can customize storage based on your items. Glass doors on some wall cabinets can display attractive storage baskets or neatly folded linens, adding visual interest to the space.

### Open Shelving for Daily-Use Items

While closed cabinets provide concealed storage, open shelving works well for items you use daily and want to keep visible and accessible. Dryer sheets, lint rollers, stain removers, and fabric fresheners are good candidates for open shelving. Open shelves also make a space feel larger and less cluttered—provided the items on them are neatly organized and visually appealing.

Open shelving works best when combined with attractive storage containers. Clear bins with labels, woven baskets, or coordinated boxes transform open shelves from cluttered to curated. Open shelving also allows for easier access and faster restocking compared to closed cabinets.

### Countertop Integration for Folding

A dedicated folding surface is transformative for laundry room functionality. A countertop at standard kitchen height (36 inches) or slightly higher (38-40 inches) reduces back strain during folding. A 24-30 inch deep countertop provides enough space to fold a full load of laundry without items falling off. A 36-48 inch wide countertop accommodates multiple tasks simultaneously—folding on one end, sorting on the other.

Countertops should be durable and moisture-resistant. Laminate, solid surface, or sealed wood all work well. Avoid porous materials that absorb moisture and stains. Consider a countertop with a slight lip or edge to prevent items from sliding off during folding.

## The Moisture Factor: Material Matters in Laundry Rooms

Cabinet material selection is the single most important decision in laundry room design. The wrong material will deteriorate quickly, requiring replacement within 3-5 years. The right material will last 15-20+ years with minimal maintenance. Understanding the moisture performance of different materials is essential.

### Why Standard MDF Fails in Laundry Rooms

Medium-density fiberboard (MDF) is an economical cabinet material made from wood fibers bonded with resin. It's affordable, stable, and works well in dry environments. However, MDF is fundamentally porous—it absorbs moisture like a sponge. In a laundry room, MDF exposed to humidity will swell, warp, and eventually delaminate. The edges are particularly vulnerable; even a thin veneer can't fully protect the porous core. Within 2-3 years, MDF cabinets in laundry rooms typically show visible damage: swollen edges, peeling veneer, and warped doors. This is why budget-friendly MDF cabinets are a false economy in laundry rooms—you'll replace them sooner than you would higher-quality alternatives.

### Engineered Wood with TFL Finish: The KLOVO Approach

Engineered wood with a thermofused laminate (TFL) finish represents a significant step up from standard MDF. TFL is a thin plastic laminate fused directly to the wood surface under heat and pressure, creating a sealed, moisture-resistant barrier. Unlike traditional veneer, which can peel and separate, TFL is permanently bonded to the substrate. This creates a cabinet that resists humidity, splashes, and stains far better than standard MDF.

KLOVO cabinets utilize engineered wood with TFL finish specifically designed for moisture-prone environments. The sealed surfaces resist humidity absorption, preventing the swelling and warping that plagues standard MDF. Splashes from detergent bottles or washing machine overflow don't penetrate the surface; they simply wipe away. The TFL finish is also highly durable—it resists scratches, scuffs, and impact damage better than paint or veneer. This combination of moisture resistance and durability makes TFL-finished engineered wood an ideal choice for laundry rooms.

Beyond moisture resistance, KLOVO cabinets feature 500-pound per-cabinet capacity, supporting heavy detergent bottles, cleaning supplies, and laundry baskets without sagging. The GlideLock assembly system allows homeowners to assemble and install cabinets without hiring a contractor, reducing installation costs and timelines. The modular design means you can start with a basic configuration and expand as your needs evolve.

### Steel Cabinets: Rust Concerns

Steel cabinets offer excellent durability and strength, but they present a specific challenge in laundry rooms: rust. Even powder-coated steel can develop rust if the coating is scratched or if moisture reaches the underlying metal. In a humid laundry room environment, rust can develop surprisingly quickly. Steel cabinets require careful maintenance—any scratches must be touched up immediately, and the space must be well-ventilated to minimize humidity. For most homeowners, the maintenance burden makes steel cabinets less practical than engineered wood alternatives.

### Solid Wood: Warping Risk

Solid wood cabinets are beautiful and can last for decades in dry environments. However, solid wood is susceptible to warping and swelling when exposed to humidity fluctuations. A laundry room's humid environment causes solid wood to absorb moisture, swell, and potentially warp. Doors may not close properly, and joints may separate. While high-end solid wood cabinets with professional finishing can perform adequately in laundry rooms, they require excellent ventilation and careful humidity control. For most homeowners, the cost and maintenance requirements make solid wood less practical than engineered wood alternatives.

Wire shelving represents the most budget-friendly option. Basic metal wire shelves with adjustable brackets cost $100-300 for a complete system. ## Frequently Asked Questions About Laundry Room Cabinets

### Q1: Can I Install Cabinets Above My Washer and Dryer?

**Answer:** Yes, but with important caveats. Wall cabinets can be installed above front-loading washers and dryers, provided they're securely mounted to wall studs and positioned high enough to allow access to the appliances. However, avoid installing cabinets directly above top-loading washers—the lid swing requires clearance. For stacked washer-dryer units, shallow wall cabinets (8-10 inches deep) work well above the stack, providing storage without interfering with appliance access. Ensure adequate ventilation around dryers to prevent heat and moisture buildup. If your dryer vents through the wall, position cabinets to avoid blocking the vent. When in doubt, consult the appliance manufacturer's guidelines and consider hiring a professional installer to ensure safety and proper clearance.

### Q2: What Cabinet Depth Works Best for Laundry Rooms?

**Answer:** Cabinet depth depends on the cabinet type and location. Base cabinets are typically 24 inches deep, matching standard kitchen cabinet depth. This depth provides ample storage for detergent bottles, cleaning supplies, and laundry baskets. Wall cabinets are typically 12-15 inches deep, providing storage for linens and supplies without protruding excessively into the room. Over-washer cabinets are often shallower (8-10 inches deep) to avoid interfering with appliance access. Folding countertops should be 24-30 inches deep to provide adequate workspace. Deeper countertops (30+ inches) are ideal if space allows, providing room for folding and sorting simultaneously. Consider your room dimensions and workflow when selecting cabinet depths—deeper cabinets provide more storage but consume more floor space.

### Q3: How Do I Protect Cabinets from Laundry Room Humidity?

**Answer:** Start with moisture-resistant materials—engineered wood with TFL finish (like KLOVO cabinets) is specifically designed to resist humidity. Beyond material selection, proper ventilation is critical. Ensure your laundry room has adequate ventilation: a bathroom exhaust fan vented to the outside, or a dedicated laundry room exhaust fan. Run the exhaust fan during and for 20-30 minutes after laundry cycles to remove moisture. Avoid blocking vents with cabinets or other items. Maintain reasonable humidity levels (30-50%) through proper ventilation and air circulation. Wipe up spills immediately to prevent moisture from seeping into cabinet seams. Avoid placing cabinets directly against exterior walls in cold climates, where condensation can form. Consider applying a moisture-resistant sealant to cabinet edges and joints for additional protection. Finally, ensure your washer and dryer are properly maintained and not leaking—a small leak can cause significant moisture damage over time.

**Answer:** Garage cabinets and laundry room cabinets serve different purposes and have different requirements. Garage cabinets are typically designed for dry storage of tools, equipment, and seasonal items. While some garage cabinets are moisture-resistant, they're not specifically engineered for the humid, splash-prone environment of a laundry room. Using garage cabinets in a laundry room is possible but not ideal. The primary concern is moisture resistance—standard garage cabinets may not have adequate protection against humidity and splashes. Additionally, garage cabinets often feature open shelving or wire racks, which aren't ideal for storing detergents and cleaning supplies that you want to keep organized and accessible.
