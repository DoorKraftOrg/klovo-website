Most garage cabinet purchases go wrong before the first screw is turned.

Not because people choose the wrong size. Not because they pick the wrong layout. They go wrong because the buyer focused on price and appearance — and nobody told them to ask about the substrate.

This guide covers the four questions that actually determine whether your garage cabinets last two years or twenty. They're not complicated. But most cabinet listings won't answer them upfront, which is exactly why it's worth knowing what to ask.

---

## 1. What is the cabinet box made of?

This is the most important question, and it's the one most people never ask.

Garage cabinet boxes — the structural panels that form the sides, top, bottom, and back — are made from one of three materials:

**Particleboard** is sawdust and wood chips compressed with adhesive. It's cheap, it machines cleanly, and it looks fine in photos. It also swells when it absorbs moisture, loses screw-holding strength as it ages, and sags under sustained weight. In a climate-controlled kitchen, particleboard performs adequately. In a garage — where temperatures swing 40+ degrees between summer and winter and humidity comes with every rainstorm — it degrades. Not dramatically, not all at once, but consistently.

**Plywood** is layers of wood veneer glued in alternating grain directions. It's dimensionally stable, holds screws well, and handles moisture far better than particleboard. The downside is cost — plywood cabinets typically run 30–50% more than particleboard equivalents.

**Engineered wood with TFL (Thermally Fused Laminate)** is the material used in high-end kitchen cabinetry and — when properly constructed — the best choice for garages. A dense MDF or HDF core is bonded under heat and pressure to a melamine or laminate surface. The result is a panel that resists moisture, holds its shape under load, and takes hard daily use without showing it. Scratch-resistant, wipe-clean, and dimensionally stable across temperature swings.

**What to look for:** Ask specifically whether the box panels are particleboard, plywood, or engineered wood with TFL. If the listing doesn't say, or just says "wood," assume particleboard.

## 2. What is the edge banding, and where is it applied?

Edge banding is the thin strip of material bonded to the exposed edges of cabinet panels — the front face of a shelf, the top of a side panel, the door perimeter.

In a garage, exposed panel edges are the entry point for moisture. A raw particleboard or MDF edge will wick humidity and begin to swell. Even engineered wood panels need proper edge treatment to perform in garage conditions.

**PVC edge banding** (also called ABS or thermoplastic edge banding) is the standard for cabinets built to last. It's bonded with hot-melt adhesive under pressure. A quality installation uses 1mm or thicker PVC on all exposed edges — doors, drawers, shelves, and side panels alike.

**Thin paper or foil edge banding** is what you'll find on budget cabinets. It peels. It chips. It lets moisture in at every joint.

**What to look for:** PVC edge banding, 1mm minimum, applied to all exposed edges. If a manufacturer mentions edge banding at all, it's usually because theirs is worth mentioning. If the spec sheet is silent on edge banding, it's probably paper.

## 3. How are the hinges and drawer slides rated?

Cabinet hardware fails in predictable ways. Hinges fatigue. Drawer slides develop play. The relevant spec is cycle rating — the number of open/close cycles a hinge or slide is tested to before showing wear.

Budget hinges: 25,000–50,000 cycles. That sounds like a lot until you calculate it. A garage cabinet used twice a day reaches 50,000 cycles in 68 years. Used ten times a day — a busy household, sports gear, kids' stuff — you're there in 14 years. Budget hinges rarely make it that far.

Soft-close hinges and full-extension drawer slides rated to 100,000+ cycles are the standard for kitchen-grade cabinetry and the right benchmark for garage cabinets that get real use.

**What to look for:** Soft-close hinges and full-extension drawer slides. Ask for the cycle rating if it isn't listed. A manufacturer confident in their hardware will tell you.

---

## 4. What does the warranty actually cover?

Warranty language tells you a lot about how a manufacturer thinks about their product.

"Limited warranty" is almost universal. What matters is what it covers and for how long. Look specifically for:

- **Duration:** 1 year is the minimum for a quality product. Less than that is a signal.
- **What's covered:** Defects in materials and workmanship. Vague language like "structural defects only" excludes the failures most likely to happen — delamination, swelling, hardware failure.
- **Who handles claims:** Some manufacturers route warranty claims through the retailer, which adds friction and delays. A direct warranty — contact the manufacturer — is cleaner and faster.
